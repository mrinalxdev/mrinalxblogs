## Day 1: 2024-12-11

#### Q. Derive the formula for the derivative of the inverse sine function, i.e. find $\frac{d}{dx} (\sin^{-1}x)$

**Solution**: The inverse sine function, denoted by $\sin^{-1}x$ is defined as:

$$
y = \sin^{-1} x \iff \sin y = x
$$

Using implicit differentiation to differentiate with respect to $x$:

* Using implicit differentiation to differentiate with respect to $x$:
  $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin y) = \frac{d}{dx} x$$

* Applying chain rule to the left-hand side:
  $$\cos y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1$$

* Solving $\frac{dy}{dx}$ we get:
  $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\cos y}$$

* Expressing $\cos y$ in terms of $x$ using Pythagorean theorem:
  $$\cos y = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 y} = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$$

* Substituting the expression for $\cos y$ into the derivative, we get:
  $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}$$

Hence proved: $$\boxed{\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}}$$

**We can Simplify even more**:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \frac{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}{1 - x^2}
$$

## Day 2: 2024-12-12

#### Q. Prove that $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$

##### Proof: We use the Squeeze Theorem to prove this.

The **Squeeze Theorem** states:

If $f(x) \leq g(x) \leq h(x)$ for all $x$ in some interval around $a$ (except possibly at $a$ itself), and

$$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L, $$

then

$$ \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = L. $$

- Step 1: Define the inequality bounds

For $x \neq 0$, consider the unit circle. From geometry, we know:

$$ \cos x \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq 1 \quad \text{for } x > 0 $$

- Step 2: Take the limit of the bounds as $x \to 0$

As $x \to 0$:

1. $\lim_{x \to 0} \cos x = 1$  
2. $\lim_{x \to 0} 1 = 1$

Thus, by the Squeeze Theorem: $$ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 $$

- Step 3: Justify using the geometric interpretation

Using the geometry of the unit circle:
- The length of the arc is $x$ (in radians).
- The vertical projection is $\sin x$.
- The horizontal projection gives $\cos x$.

By comparing the areas and relationships geometrically, the inequality holds.

#### Final Answer:

Thus, we have proved that: $$ \boxed{\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1} $$

## Day 3: 2024-12-13

#### Q. Find the derivative of the function : $f(x) = \frac{x^3 e^x}{(1 + x^2)^2}$

**Solution** According to quotient rule which stats : $$ \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \frac{u(x)}{v(x)} \right] = \frac{u'(x)v(x) - u(x)v'(x)}{v(x)^2}. $$

Given : $u(x) = x^3 e^x$ & $v(x) = (1 + x^2)^2$ --(i)

1. $u' (x) = \frac{d}{dx}[(1 + x^2)^2]$
    - $\frac{d}{dx}[x^3 e^3] = 3x^2 e^x + x^3 e^x.$ Used product rule
    - $$ \boxed{u'(x) = e^x(3x^2 + x^3)} $$

2. $v'(x) = \frac{d}{dx}[(1 + x^2)^2]$
    - Using chain rule $$ \frac{d}{dx}[(1 + x^2)^2] = 2(1 + x^2) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[1 + x^2] = 2(1 + x^2)(2x). $$
    - $$ \boxed{v'(x) = 4x(1 + x^2)} $$

Substituting $u(x)$, $u'(x)$, $v(x)$, and $v'(x)$ into (i)

### $$ \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \frac{x^3 e^x}{(1 + x^2)^2} \right] = \frac{u'(x)v(x) - u(x)v'(x)}{v(x)^2}. $$

Substitute the terms:

- $u'(x) = e^x(3x^2 + x^3)$
- $v(x) = (1 + x^2)^2$
- $u(x) = x^3 e^x$
- $v'(x) = 4x(1 + x^2)$

Thus:

### $$ \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \frac{x^3 e^x}{(1 + x^2)^2} \right] = \frac{e^x(3x^2 + x^3)(1 + x^2)^2 - x^3 e^x \cdot 4x(1 + x^2)}{(1 + x^2)^4}. $$

Simplifying the numerator

$$ \text{Numerator} = e^x \left[ (3x^2 + x^3)(1 + x^2)^2 - 4x^4(1 + x^2) \right]. $$

#### Final Answer:

$$ \boxed{\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \frac{x^3 e^x}{(1 + x^2)^2} \right] = \frac{e^x \left[ (3x^2 + x^3)(1 + x^2)^2 - 4x^4(1 + x^2) \right]}{(1 + x^2)^4}} $$

## Day 4 : 2024-12-14

#### Q. Prove that: $$ \int \frac{x^2}{(1 + x^2)^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \ln(1 + x^2) + \frac{1}{2(1 + x^2)} + C. $$
<br />
**Solution** : Let $u = 1 + x^2$. Then: $$ \frac{du}{dx} = 2x \quad \text{or} \quad du = 2x dx. $$

- Rewriting the integral in terms of $u$: $$ \int \frac{x^2}{(1 + x^2)^2} dx = \int \frac{x^2}{u^2} \cdot \frac{du}{2x}. $$

- Simplifying : $$ \int \frac{x^2}{(1 + x^2)^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{x}{u^2} du. $$

- From $u = 1 + x^2$, we have $x^2 = u - 1$.

- Substituting: $$ \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{x^2}{u^2} du = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{u - 1}{u^2} du. $$

- Splitting the fraction : $$ \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{u - 1}{u^2} du = \frac{1}{2} \ln|u| + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{u}. $$

- Replacing $u$ with $1 + x^2$: $$ \frac{1}{2} \ln|1 + x^2| + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{1 + x^2}. $$

- Hence : 

$$ \boxed{\int \frac{x^2}{(1 + x^2)^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \ln(1 + x^2) + \frac{1}{2(1 + x^2)} + C.}$$ **proved**

## Day 5 : 2024-12-15

#### Q. Prove and Evaluate $$\int \frac{x^3}{(1 + x^2)^3} \, dx = -\frac{x^2}{2(1 + x^2)^2} + \frac{1}{4(1 + x^2)} + C$$

**Solution** : The given integral involves a rational function. To simplify it, observe the denominator $$(1 + x^2)^3$$ and note that $$x^3$$ in the numerator suggests a substitution related to $$1 + x^2$$.

- Let : $$u = 1 + x^2 \quad \text{so that} \quad du = 2x \, dx.$$

- From $$ u = 1 + x^2$$ it follows that $$x^2 = u - 1$$. Rewriting $$x^3$$ as $$x \cdot x^2$$

- ### $$\int \frac{x^3}{(1 + x^2)^3} \, dx = \int \frac{x \cdot (u - 1)}{u^3} \cdot \frac{du}{2x}.$$

- Simplifying : $$\int \frac{x^3}{(1 + x^2)^3} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{u - 1}{u^3} \, du.$$

- Splitting up the fraction becomes : $$\frac{u - 1}{u^3} = \frac{u}{u^3} - \frac{1}{u^3} = \frac{1}{u^2} - \frac{1}{u^3}.$$

- ### $$\frac{1}{2} \int \left( \frac{1}{u^2} - \frac{1}{u^3} \right) \, du.$$

- For $$\int \frac{1}{u^2} \, du$$ : $$\int \frac{1}{u^2} \, du = \int u^{-2} \, du = \frac{u^{-1}}{-1} = -\frac{1}{u}.$$

- Substitute these results back into the integral: $$\frac{1}{2} \int \left( \frac{1}{u^2} - \frac{1}{u^3} \right) \, du = \frac{1}{2} \left( -\frac{1}{u} + \frac{1}{2u^2} \right).$$

- Substitute back $$u = 1 + x^2$$ : $$\frac{1}{2} \left( -\frac{1}{u} + \frac{1}{2u^2} \right) = \frac{1}{2} \left( -\frac{1}{1 + x^2} + \frac{1}{2(1 + x^2)^2} \right).$$

- Distributing $$\frac{1}{2}$$ : $$-\frac{1}{2(1 + x^2)} + \frac{1}{4(1 + x^2)^2}.$$

Including the constant of the integration

$$\boxed{\int \frac{x^3}{(1 + x^2)^3} \, dx = -\frac{x^2}{2(1 + x^2)^2} + \frac{1}{4(1 + x^2)} + C.}$$ **Hence proved..**
